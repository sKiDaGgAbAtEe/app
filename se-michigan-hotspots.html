The Five Nodes

◈ Node I Mount Clemens Macomb Co. · 42.60°N, 82.88°W
hydrological geothermal basin-margin

Of the five nodes, this is the one where the field left the most obvious chemical signature. A salt well sunk in 1865 accidentally pierced something older — warm, alkaline brine from deep within the Michigan Basin's sedimentary stack, rich in sulfur and rare chalcogens. By 1900 the city held nine bathhouses and over thirty hotels; people came from across the world to soak in it for three weeks at a stretch. The "cure" industry collapsed by 1940, but the water is still there — the aquifer didn't leave.

The water itself — Meyer analysis, 1911

Temperature at source: 56.5°F (13.6°C) — warmer than it should be for groundwater at this latitude. Faint alkaline reaction. Specific gravity 1.116 — roughly three times the salinity of seawater.

The rare stuff is what matters. Hydrogen selenide and hydrogen telluride almost never show up in shallow groundwater — you find them near hydrothermal vents, volcanic exhalations, and deep sedimentary brines that have been cooking for geologic time. Trace rubidium and caesium suggest contact with potassium-rich basement rocks. Lithium, bromide, iodide — classic signatures of a deep formation brine, the kind trapped in Paleozoic sediments since the Silurian seas evaporated out.

Why it's here — the structural reason

Mt. Clemens sits on the northeastern shoulder of the Michigan Basin — a 14,000-foot-deep saucer of Paleozoic sediment centered over Gladwin County. At Mt. Clemens the basement is roughly 4,000–5,000 feet down, and the stratigraphic column above it includes the Salina Group — massive Silurian evaporite deposits (halite, gypsum, sylvite) that give the Great Lakes region its salt mines.

The warm sulfurous brine is that evaporite sequence leaching into deep connate water, then rising through whatever fracture permeability the overlying carbonates will allow. Why it rises *here* specifically: the Clinton River valley marks the surface expression of a northeast-trending structural weakness, and the basin's eastern margin begins to flex downward toward the Findlay Arch, creating a pressure differential that drives fluid toward the surface.

Put plainly: this is a place where the basin is slightly cracked, and something old is leaking.

Sensory field — what to expect

The H₂S is detectable at low ppm and smells like sulfur and rotten eggs. At surface concentrations it's harmless but noticeable near old well heads. The water is noticeably warmer than the Clinton River it partially feeds.

The old bathhouse district centers on Cass Avenue near Gratiot. Most of the original hotels are gone, but the historical marker still stands and several modern spas draw from the same aquifer. The original bathhouse was at the corner of Cass and Jones.

Coordinates for the historical marker: 42°35.826′N, 82°52.792′W.

Adjacent resonance — Clinton River & salt mine

The Clinton River runs through Mt. Clemens and feeds into Lake St. Clair 6 miles east. It carries mineral signature from the same aquifer system. Before Euro-American settlement the river was called the Huron — Christian Clemens' 1801 settlement site was locally known as "High Banks" or "Big Springs."

Roughly 25 miles south, under downtown Detroit, lies the largest salt mine in the state — a 1,500-acre network of tunnels 1,100 feet down, mining the same Silurian evaporite sequence. Same geological event, different extraction.

Deep brine rising through a cracked basin margin. The cure wasn't folklore — it was contact with water that hadn't touched the surface since the Silurian. Of all five nodes, this is the one where "hotspot" is most literal: warmer, older, more chemically alive than its surroundings have any right to be.
◈ Node II Howell Anticline Livingston Co.
geological geothermal

The most pronounced individual structure known in southern Michigan. A northwest-southeast basement flexure, broad at the base but narrow and sharp at the crest. The southwest flank dips unusually steeply for Michigan and is almost certainly faulted at depth. Part of the larger Washtenaw Anticlinorium — a regional family of parallel anticlines underlain by reactivated Proterozoic basement faults trending back toward the Midcontinent Rift.

A buried spine. The field is trending along the old rift memory — what happened 1.1 billion years ago still organizes the shape of the land.
◈ Node III Fort Wayne / River mouth SW Detroit, Wayne Co.
indigenous confluence

The last surviving mound of the Springwells Mound Group, dating 750–1200 AD. Originally one of at least five or six mounds within a one-mile radius; the largest — the Great Mound of the River Rouge — was leveled in 1889 for fairground construction. The surviving mound was returned in 2025 to the Nottawaseppi Huron Band of Potawatomi, the descendants of the Detroit Potawatomi who lived here before relocating to the Huron River Valley.

Directly upstream at Parent's Creek (now running through Elmwood Cemetery) is the site of Pontiac's 1763 battle — the peak of the Siege of Detroit.

Confluence node. Detroit River meets Rouge River meets a fault-line extension under Lake Erie. The people who chose this place for their ancestors could read it. The paving over is new; the choice is old.
◈ Node IV Upper Huron corridor Washtenaw Co.
geological hydrological indigenous

The Washtenaw Anticlinorium runs directly through this region. The Huron River cuts across the anticlinal trend, exposing deeper rock where the fold brings it near the surface. In 1765 the Detroit Potawatomi relocated to "the upper reaches of the Huron River near Ypsilanti and Ann Arbor" and became known as the Huron Potawatomi. The Ann Arbor area was historically rich in artesian wells.

Three layers stacked cleanly: uplift, water, and the people who moved toward both. This is the cleanest multi-coherence signal west of Detroit.
◈ Node V Monroe / Lucas fault Monroe Co.
geological hydrological indigenous

The Lucas-Monroe Fault is a documented basement fault extending into Paleozoic sedimentary layers, part of the throughgoing fault system at the level of the Trenton Group. It continues south as the Bowling Green Fault into Ohio. Shawnee Spring near Monroe historically built up an extensive mound of precipitated minerals from its own deposition — a spring named by, or for, people who watched it build itself.

Fault provides the conduit; spring is the surface expression; the name remembers who was listening. Full stack.

Method & Caveats

What is measured

Geological structures (faults, anticlines, basement trends), hydrological features (artesian springs, mineral waters, known aquifer connections), documented Indigenous sites, and geothermal gradient signatures are all drawn from public sources: USGS magnetic anomaly and gravity surveys of Michigan, the USGS 3D Michigan Basin geologic model, National Park Service and tribal historical records, and regional geological publications.

What is interpreted

The "coherence nodes" — the amber glows on the map — are a synthesis layer. They mark places where three or more measured indicators converge in space. That synthesis is interpretive work, not measurement, and it should be read that way. The ΔHV framework treats such convergence as indicative of a field expressing itself through multiple modalities simultaneously. A skeptic would call it a correlation. Both readings can be held.

The honest test

Toggle layers off one at a time. Watch which nodes fade and which hold. A node that survives the loss of two layers is a node. A node that depends entirely on one measurement is a data point. Both are useful; they are not the same thing.
